CHAPTER 01 USA EU

Buying & Flipping Distressed Debt Portfolios

▲ Potential IRR: 50%–200%+ ● Entry Capital: $5,000+

Banks and credit card companies write off billions in bad debt annually. Rather than chase it forever, they sell it to investors at 1–14 cents per dollar. You collect what you can — and pocket the spread.

Debt buying is a $13.7 billion industry in the USA alone. The core mechanic is simple: a creditor — say, a credit card issuer — charges off an account they can no longer collect and bundles it with thousands of similar accounts into a portfolio. They sell this portfolio to a debt buyer for pennies on the dollar. The buyer then either collects directly, outsources collection to a licensed agency, or flips the portfolio onward at a higher price.

According to FTC data from a landmark study, nine of the largest debt buyers purchased 76.1% of total US debt, with portfolios traded at 3–20 cents per dollar depending on age and origin. That gap is your profit engine.

As a passive debt buyer (flipper), your goal is not to call debtors — it's to buy portfolios cheap and resell them for more to active collectors who have infrastructure to pursue collection. You earn arbitrage without running a call centre.

📊 Real Data — FTC Study (6 Major Debt Buyers)

Portfolios analysed: 5,000+ | Consumer accounts: ~90 million | Face value: ~$143 billion | Purchase price paid: ~$6.5 billion. That is 4.5 cents per dollar. Firms achieving 20–40% collection rates net returns of 3–9x their acquisition cost.

Source: FTC — Structure and Practices of the Debt Buying Industry (ftc.gov)

"A debt buyer pays $1,000 for $10,000 of charged-off debt. Everything collected above $1,000 is profit."

Step-by-Step Entry Plan

01

Get Licensed (USA)

Most states require a debt collector licence to actively collect — but as a passive buyer/reseller, requirements vary. Check your state's requirements via your Attorney General's office. Some states require no licence for simple portfolio resale.

02

Start Small on a Debt Marketplace

Buy a low-volume portfolio starting at $5,000–$15,000 face value on an exchange. Get your feet wet, understand portfolio scoring, and learn to assess collectability before committing larger capital.

03

Score the Portfolio — Know What You're Buying

Assess debt age (fresh < 1 year is best), original creditor type, geographic concentration, documentation completeness, and prior collection attempts. Older debt = cheaper but harder to collect.

04

Flip to a Licensed Collector or Operate via a Third Party

As a passive buyer, negotiate with a licensed collection agency to work the accounts on a revenue-split basis (typically 25–40% to the agency). You keep the rest.

05

Scale — Join the Receivables Management Association International

Network with other buyers and sellers. Access larger deal flow. Industry organisations open doors to direct creditor relationships, bypassing the marketplace middleman entirely.

CHAPTER 02 USA

Tax Lien & Tax Deed Investing

▲ Returns: 8%–36% annually (or property at deep discount) ● Entry Capital: $500–$50,000+

When a property owner fails to pay their taxes, the US government doesn't just write it off — it sells the right to collect that debt (with interest) to private investors at public auction. This is one of America's most overlooked fixed-income strategies.

Currently, 30 US states plus Washington, D.C. allow private investors to purchase tax lien certificates. The government reclaims its tax revenue immediately; you become the lienholder and earn statutory interest — set by state law — until the owner pays you back, which most do.

Interest rates vary wildly by state: Florida pays 18%, Iowa 24%, Arizona 16%, Illinois 36%. If the owner never pays, after the redemption period (typically 1–3 years), you can initiate foreclosure — potentially acquiring a property worth far more than your lien purchase price.

The alternative — tax deed investing — skips the waiting period. In tax deed states, the county auctions the property itself after delinquency. Savvy investors have acquired $100,000 properties for $30,000–$40,000 at these auctions and flipped them for near-market value immediately.

📊 Real Case Study — Tax Deed Investor

Property auctioned in a tax deed state. Opening bid: $9,000. Final purchase: $30,000. Legal costs to convert to warranty deed: $5,000. Property estimated value: $100,000. Sold quickly at: $75,000. Net profit: ~$40,000 on $35,000 invested — doubling capital in 6–12 months.

01

Choose Your State Strategically

Pick a lien state with high statutory interest. Illinois (36%) and Iowa (24%) offer outstanding guaranteed returns if the owner redeems. Competition is lower in rural counties — go where institutional money isn't looking.

02

Find Auctions on County Websites

Every county treasurer's site lists upcoming tax sales. You can also use platforms like DealMachine, Bid4Assets, or PropertyOnion to find and track auctions without manual county-by-county research.

03

Due Diligence on the Property

Research the property's estimated market value, condition, and any senior liens (IRS liens can wipe out tax liens). Use county GIS maps, Google Street View, and public records. Never bid blind.

04

Bid at Auction & Collect

Online auctions are common — register, fund an account, and bid. If you win, pay the full certificate amount. Then sit back and earn interest until the owner redeems — which happens the vast majority of the time.

CHAPTER 03 Global

Unclaimed Money & Government Surplus Recovery

▲ Pure Profit: Commission or Finder's Fee ● Entry Capital: Near Zero

Governments across the USA, UK, and EU are sitting on billions in unclaimed property, forgotten pension entitlements, dormant bank accounts, and tax overpayments — legally owed to individuals who don't know it exists. You can get paid to find it for them.

The US holds over $70 billion in unclaimed property across state treasuries. The UK's financial services sector holds hundreds of millions in dormant accounts. Every EU country has mandatory dormant account transfer laws. This money is publicly searchable — but most people never look.

The business model: you act as a heir finder or asset recovery specialist. You search public databases, identify beneficiaries of unclaimed estates or dormant funds, contact them, and take a legally agreed commission (typically 15–40%) for recovering funds they would have never found. No licence is required in most US states for this work — though some states cap finder's fees.

A more sophisticated version targets unclaimed estate probate: in the UK, the Treasury Solicitor publishes lists of people who died without a known heir (bona vacantia). You research genealogy, locate the legal heir, and charge a commission. Professional heir hunters earn six figures annually running this as their primary business.

📊 Scale of the Opportunity

USA: $70B+ in unclaimed property held by state governments. Each state transfers dormant accounts annually. Only ~$3B is returned to rightful owners per year — leaving the vast majority sitting unclaimed.

UK: HMRC holds billions in unclaimed tax refunds and the Bona Vacantia list is updated continuously with new estates.

CHAPTER 04 Global

Aged Domain Name Arbitrage

▲ Returns: 10x–1,000x on premium names ● Entry Capital: $10–$2,000 per domain

Domain names expire every day. Some of them have thousands of backlinks, established SEO authority, and brand potential — and they go back to public availability for $10. Knowing how to find and flip them is a genuine arbitrage hiding in plain sight.

Domain investors (called "domainers") buy expired or expiring domains with existing SEO value, traffic, or brand potential — and resell them to businesses at 10–1,000x the acquisition price. A domain bought for $12 can sell for $12,000 to the right buyer.

Three distinct plays exist: (1) Acquire expired domains for SEO — domains with legacy backlinks are gold for web publishers who use them as "link farms" or redirect their authority to new sites. (2) Find exact-match keyword domains and sell to businesses in that niche. (3) Buy .eu, .de, .fr, or .co.uk equivalents of popular global brands or generic keywords and flip to European buyers.

Tools like ExpiredDomains.net show hundreds of thousands of expiring domains daily with their SEO metrics. The skill is in filtering — using Moz, Ahrefs, or Majestic to identify domains with real authority and not just spam metrics.

📊 Market Reality — GoDaddy Auctions Data

GoDaddy's auction platform sells thousands of domains per month. Premium domains (3–5 letter, exact-match, or high-DA expired domains) regularly sell for $500–$50,000. The record domain sale (Voice.com) closed at $30 million. The secondary market for aged domains turns over hundreds of millions annually.

CHAPTER 05 USA UK

Storage Unit Auctions & Resale

▲ Average ROI: 2x–10x on winning units ● Entry Capital: $50–$2,000 per unit

When people stop paying their storage rent, operators auction off the contents. Savvy buyers find antiques, electronics, jewellery, and collectibles — then resell them on eBay, Facebook Marketplace, or at flea markets for multiples of the auction price.

The US storage auction industry generates over $1 billion in auction revenue annually. The TV show Storage Wars popularised it — but the real money is made by people who show up consistently, read units correctly, and have solid resale channels. In the UK, self-storage operators run similar auctions through SelfStorageAuctions.co.uk and local auctioneers.

The edge is in knowing what to look for: furniture in good condition, sealed boxes (suggesting organised storage of valuables), tools, musical instruments, and branded electronics all command strong resale value. Avoid units with clothes mountains, mattresses, and broken furniture — they cost more to dispose of than they're worth.

The professional model: buy 2–5 units per week, sort aggressively, list high-value items on eBay, mid-value on Facebook Marketplace, and bulk-sell the remainder as a lot to a flea market vendor. Total time to clear a unit: 10–15 hours. Profit per unit: $200–$2,000.

CHAPTER 06 EU UK

Invoice Factoring Arbitrage

▲ Typical Returns: 1%–5% per invoice (monthly equivalent 12%–60% annualised) ● Entry Capital: €10,000+

Small businesses are desperate for cash flow — they have invoices due in 60–90 days but need money now. As a private factoring investor, you buy their invoices at a discount and collect the full amount when the invoice is paid.

Invoice factoring is a centuries-old financial technique. A small business has an invoice for €20,000 due in 60 days. Rather than wait, they sell it to you for €19,000. You collect the full €20,000 from the debtor — profiting €1,000 in 60 days on your €19,000 investment. That's 5.3% in two months — over 30% annualised.

Modern platforms have democratised this. MarketInvoice (now Bibby Financial), Kriya Finance, and platforms like Defacto in France allow private investors to participate in invoice funding with as little as €1,000–€5,000. Platforms vet the businesses, validate invoices, and handle collection — you simply allocate capital.

The EU's Late Payment Directive (2011/7/EU) and its upcoming revision strengthens the legal standing of invoice holders, reducing your recovery risk. This is particularly powerful in Germany, France, and the Netherlands where B2B payment norms are well-established and legally enforceable.

📊 Platform Data — MarketInvoice / Kriya Finance (UK)

Over £5 billion in invoices funded. Average advance rate: 90% of invoice value. Average tenure: 55 days. Default rate on invoices historically under 0.5% (debtor risk, not SME risk — you're owed by the large corporation that ordered from the SME).

CHAPTER 07 USA EU

Government Contract Subcontracting

▲ Margins: 15%–40% on subcontracted work ● Entry Capital: Near Zero (service business)

Governments award billions in contracts to prime contractors — who then need to subcontract significant portions. You don't win the government contract. You win the subcontract. And you do the same to others below you.

US federal law (the Small Business Act) actually requires prime contractors to subcontract portions of large federal contracts to small businesses. This is mandated — meaning large primes are actively looking for qualified subcontractors. The US government spends over $600 billion annually in contracts.

The play: register as a small business contractor (SAM.gov in the USA, TED.europa.eu in the EU), identify prime contractors winning large awards in your field of expertise, and approach them offering to fulfil a defined scope of work. You mark up the actual work by 20–35%.

In the EU, the Public Procurement Directive (2014/24/EU) explicitly encourages splitting contracts for SMEs and requires buyers to justify when they do not. French, German, and Italian government purchasing portals all host subcontracting networks. Many professionals — in IT, consulting, food services, translation, and facilities — earn more subcontracting government work than they ever did directly.

CHAPTER 08 Global

Credit Card Churning & Sign-Up Bonus Harvesting

▲ Value Generated: $2,000–$15,000/year in travel & cash ● Entry Requirement: Good credit score (700+)

Credit card companies pay you to sign up. The sign-up bonuses on premium cards are worth hundreds of dollars each. Done systematically, this is a recurring, tax-efficient income stream of points, miles, and cash.

Churning is the practice of opening a credit card, meeting the minimum spend requirement to trigger the sign-up bonus, and then either keeping the card for its ongoing benefits or closing it before the next annual fee. Done with discipline and good credit, practised churners generate $5,000–$15,000 in annual value in flights, hotel stays, and cash.

In the USA, cards like the Chase Sapphire Preferred offer 60,000 points (worth ~$750), the Amex Platinum gives 80,000+ Membership Rewards points, and the Capital One Venture X offers 75,000 miles. Each can be converted to business class flights to Europe worth $3,000–$5,000 per seat.

In the UK and EU, American Express Platinum, Barclays Avios cards, and airline-co-branded cards offer similar mechanics. The key: never carry a balance. Always pay in full. The credit card company is betting you'll pay interest. You're betting you won't — and you always win that bet.

CHAPTER 09 Global

Buying & Monetising Micro-SaaS Products

▲ Acquisition multiples: 2x–4x ARR | Upside: unlimited ● Entry Capital: $1,000–$50,000+

Founders burn out. Small profitable software tools go up for sale at 2–3x annual revenue with zero fanfare. You acquire them, add marketing, and either hold for cashflow or flip at a higher multiple.

Micro-SaaS is small software-as-a-service — tools making $500–$10,000/month in recurring revenue, often with no employees and minimal ongoing maintenance. Founders sell on marketplaces like Acquire.com or MicroAcquire for 2–4x annual revenue. A tool making $2,000/month sells for $48,000–$96,000.

The opportunity: most micro-SaaS founders are technical, not marketing-driven. Growth plateaued because no one was doing outreach, SEO, or partnerships. A buyer with marketing skills can double MRR in 6–12 months — turning a $96,000 acquisition into a $200,000+ asset. Or simply hold and collect the $24,000/year cashflow.

In the EU, platforms like Flippa and Acquire.com list European tools regularly. Look for: stable or growing MRR, low churn, monthly recurring billing (not one-time), and a simple codebase. Avoid anything dependent on a single customer (>20% revenue) or a single platform API.

CHAPTER 10 UK EU

Litigation Finance — Funding Lawsuits for Profit

▲ Returns: 20%–50%+ of settlement/judgment proceeds ● Entry Capital: £10,000+ (retail); £100K+ (institutional)

You fund someone's lawsuit. They win. You take a percentage. If they lose, you lose nothing — they owe you nothing. This is litigation finance, and it's one of the fastest-growing alternative asset classes in Europe.

Litigation funding (also called legal finance or third-party funding) is where an investor pays a plaintiff's legal costs in exchange for a share of any eventual recovery. The funder takes on the financial risk; the plaintiff retains the right to pursue justice they couldn't otherwise afford.

In the UK, litigation funding is largely unregulated and well-established. The Association of Litigation Funders (ALF) oversees a code of conduct. Major funders like Burford Capital (listed on the London Stock Exchange with a market cap of £3B+) and Harbour Litigation Funding have generated IRRs of 25–40% annually on portfolios. Retail investors can access this via listed vehicles or specialist funds.

In the EU, the Netherlands and Germany are most mature. The EU Commission has discussed regulatory frameworks (2022 Resolution) but retail access is growing via platforms like Legalist and co-investment with law firms on a case-by-case basis. Target cases: commercial disputes, IP infringement, and class actions where the claim is clear and the defendant is solvent.

📊 Burford Capital — Public Company Performance

Burford Capital (BUR on NYSE and LSE) reported an overall portfolio IRR of approximately 30% on concluded investments as of their 2024 annual report. The company manages over $5B in assets and its litigation portfolio has generated billions in returns since 2009 — making it one of the highest-returning specialty finance companies globally.

Royalty Streams — Music, Patents & Mineral Rights

▲ Ongoing Passive Income: 6%–20%+ yield on purchase price ● Entry Capital: $500 (music royalties) to $50,000+ (mineral rights)

Royalties are the closest thing to genuinely passive income: someone else does the work, and you collect a percentage forever. You can now buy royalty streams from musicians, inventors, and landowners as a standalone investment.

Music royalties pay every time a song is streamed, played on radio, used in a film, or performed live. Platforms like Royalty Exchange auction individual song catalogues and royalty streams. A track that earns $10,000/year might sell for $80,000–$120,000 (8–12x earnings). If streams grow, so does your income.

Patent royalties: inventors and small companies license patents to manufacturers who pay a percentage of sales. Patent owners often sell partial or full royalty rights to investors. Platforms like IP Advantage and Ocean Tomo broker these transactions.

Mineral rights (USA): landowners can own the rights to oil, gas, or other minerals below their land separately from the surface. These rights pay royalties whenever extraction occurs. They can be bought and sold — and in energy-producing states like Texas, Oklahoma, and North Dakota, they generate substantial passive income at yields of 10–20% on acquisition price in active areas.

CHAPTER 12 Global

Government & Bankruptcy Surplus Auctions

▲ Discounts: 50%–90% below retail ● Entry Capital: $200–$10,000 per lot

Every week, governments, police departments, customs agencies, and bankrupt businesses liquidate assets at a fraction of market value. Most people don't know these auctions exist. You will.

US federal government surplus — from military vehicles to office equipment — is liquidated through GovPlanet, PublicSurplus, and GSA Auctions. A $40,000 commercial refrigeration unit goes for $2,000. A fleet vehicle worth $25,000 sells for $4,000. The key is knowing what resale markets exist for what you buy.

Police auctions sell seized and unclaimed property — bicycles, electronics, jewellery, and vehicles — at prices 60–80% below retail. UK police auctions run through sites like Wilsons Auctions and local force-specific portals. EU customs agencies in Germany (Zoll), France (Douanes), and the Netherlands auction confiscated goods regularly.

Bankruptcy liquidations are particularly rich. When a company goes under, its assets — inventory, equipment, IP, vehicles, real estate — are sold rapidly for cash. BidSpotter and Heritage Global in the USA, and Hilco in the EU/UK, specialise in these. Resellers who build expertise in a niche (restaurant equipment, medical devices, industrial machinery) can develop predictable, profitable acquisition and resale businesses.
